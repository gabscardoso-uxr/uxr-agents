Research Questions

Primary question: Why are Gen Z women disengaging from the platform, and what would need to be true for them to stay or come back?

  1. What specific experiences or moments made them pull back — and do those map to safety, product friction, or something else entirely?
  2. How do they describe what this app is "for" now, compared to where they're spending time instead?
  3. What safety or boundary experiences have shaped how they feel about the platform — including things they never reported?
  4. What does a dating app that actually works for them look like, in their words?

The decision

We're deciding where to put next quarter's investment: safety and trust infrastructure, core product experience, or competitive repositioning. These are fundamentally different bets that require different teams, timelines, and success metrics. The risk of getting it wrong is building something that doesn't address why people are actually leaving — like adding profile features when the real problem is harassment. This decision is partially reversible (roadmap can shift quarter-over-quarter) but the reputational cost of continued inaction on safety, if that's the driver, is not.

What we think we know vs. what might actually be true

The industry assumption is that Gen Z women want more features and engagement mechanics — more ways to express themselves, more filters, more prompts. But the desk research suggests the opposite: they may be leaving because the product demands too much emotional labor, not too little. Swiping feels transactional, conversations feel scripted, and the cumulative weight of low-grade negative interactions is exhausting.

We also assume that safety features (report, block) are sufficient because they exist. But public signal strongly suggests that the experience of needing to use safety tools is itself a deterrent. Women aren't saying "I reported someone and nothing happened" — they're saying "I shouldn't have to deal with this at all." The bar isn't better reporting. It's fewer incidents.

And we assume competitive loss is about features. It may actually be about norms and culture. Apps like Hinge and Feeld are gaining ground not because they have better features, but because they feel different to use. The behavioral contract between users is different.

Hypotheses to design against

  1. Gen Z women reduce usage after repeated low-grade negative interactions (not just reportable incidents) because the cumulative cost exceeds the value of staying — which means the product needs passive safety signals, not just reactive tools.
  2. Gen Z women see the platform's brand as misaligned with how they want to present themselves socially — it's associated with behaviors they find unacceptable — which means the product's public identity may need to shift before features matter.
  3. Gen Z women aren't going to one competitor — they're fragmenting across apps with narrower use cases (Hinge for serious, Feeld for exploratory, Thursday for event-driven) — which means the design response may be depth in one use case rather than breadth.

Who we're designing for

Recently churned — had an active usage pattern, stopped or deleted within the last 90 days. These women can describe specific moments that preceded their decision. Highest signal value for this study.

At-risk / declining — usage has dropped significantly but the account is still active. They can describe what's currently pushing them away vs. what keeps them partially engaged. Good for understanding the friction-to-value ratio.

Never-activated — created an account but never reached meaningful engagement. May reveal first-impression or onboarding barriers specific to this segment. Lower priority for this study but worth including 1–2 if recruitment allows.

What people are already doing without us

We don't have direct evidence yet, but desk research and public signal point to several likely patterns: using the app in restricted ways (never initiating, only responding) as self-protection; migrating to apps perceived as safer or more intentional; and using external channels (group chats, Instagram, TikTok) to vet matches before engaging on-platform. Fieldwork must confirm which of these are real and how widespread they are — designing without this data means we're guessing at what behaviors to support or replace.

What's constrained

iOS and Android parity is non-negotiable — any safety or trust feature has to ship on both platforms simultaneously. A gap creates inconsistent user experience in exactly the area where consistency matters most. If research points to behavioral moderation (detecting patterns like repeated boundary-pushing, not just flagged content), the existing moderation stack may need architectural changes. And any new data collection for safety signals has to comply with platform privacy policies and regional regulations.

What can't be undone

Matching algorithm changes that affect all users are hard to roll back once shipped — if research points to algorithmic interventions for safety, the rollout and rollback plan needs to be defined before launch. Data schema changes for new safety signals create storage and handling obligations that are difficult to reverse. And public-facing trust and safety commitments, once communicated, become expectations that are costly to walk back.

What this study can't answer — and who can

This study will tell us what users need and how they behave, but it can't determine whether behavioral signals (message tone, interaction patterns) can be reliably detected at scale — that's an eng spike. It can't tell us whether moderation latency can meet the threshold where intervention feels timely — that's a performance assessment. And it won't resolve platform-level impact of real-time behavioral monitoring — that needs load testing and architecture review, ideally in parallel with the research.

What would change the build

If we find that drop-off is driven by repeated low-grade negative interactions rather than reportable incidents, the investment shifts from reactive tools (report/block) to passive behavioral detection and proactive intervention — a fundamentally different architecture. If it turns out users are leaving because of how the platform feels (brand, norms, culture) rather than how it works, the technical investment shifts to community norms infrastructure: onboarding flows, profile signals, behavioral nudges. And if users are fragmenting to specialized apps, we might need modular architecture that supports differentiated experiences within one app rather than one-size-fits-all.

What's already out there — desk research

There's substantial public signal on this topic. Three themes emerge consistently across Reddit threads, app store reviews, industry reporting, and academic research:

Safety and harassment fatigue is the loudest signal. Research shows sexual harassment on dating apps ranges between 57% and 89% of women, with younger women at higher risk. 57% of female users 18–34 report receiving unsolicited explicit images. But the issue isn't just acute incidents — it's the cumulative burden. Women describe exhaustion from constant low-level boundary violations that individually don't feel "bad enough" to report but collectively make the platform feel hostile. A 2025 NPR investigation found that individuals accused of assault could remain on major platforms despite multiple complaints.

Swipe fatigue and emotional labor are driving burnout. More than half of Gen Z reports feeling burned out "often or always" on dating apps — the highest of any age group. The swiping mechanic is described as transactional and superficial. Gen Z women aren't asking for more matches; they're asking for less confusion and fewer scripted conversations. Apps that emphasize intentionality (Hinge's "designed to be deleted," Thursday's event-driven model, Feeld's consent-forward design) are gaining ground specifically because they feel less like work.

The competitive landscape has fragmented. Gen Z women aren't moving to a single competitor — they're spreading across apps with narrower, more specific value propositions. Hinge for serious dating, Feeld for exploratory, Her for LGBTQ+ community, Thursday for event-driven connection. The common thread isn't features but norms — platforms where the behavioral contract between users feels different.

Gap: Public signal is strong on the industry-wide trend but thin on what's specific to this platform. Internal retention data, support tickets, and app store reviews need to confirm whether these broader patterns explain our specific drop-off or if something platform-specific is happening.

What's already inside — internal signals to review

Before fieldwork, the team should review: (1) retention cohort data for Gen Z women over the past 12 months — when and where in the user journey does drop-off accelerate; (2) Trust & Safety reports filed by Gen Z women — volume, nature, and any changes in the past 6 months; (3) app store reviews and social media mentions filtered to this segment; (4) customer support tickets — themes in complaints and account deletion reasons.

Gap: If Trust & Safety report data is not segmentable by age and gender, flag this immediately. The research design depends on knowing whether reported incidents correlate with the drop-off timeline.

Recommendation

8–10 in-depth interviews (60 min, remote) with recently churned and at-risk Gen Z women. Human-led — this is non-negotiable. The topic involves safety, harassment, and personal boundaries. Participants may disclose experiences with unwanted sexual content, stalking, or boundary violations. The facilitator needs to be trained in trauma-informed interviewing. AI tools can support scheduling, transcription, and preliminary coding, but not moderation or participant interaction.

From finding to action

If we find that safety and harassment experiences are the primary driver, the investment goes to trust and safety infrastructure — not feature development. VP Product and the Trust & Safety lead would define the safety investment track for next quarter.

If the driver is competitive positioning (brand perception, norms, culture), the problem is how we're perceived, not how we function. Marketing and Product leadership would define a repositioning strategy, and the signals to watch would be brand perception and new user acquisition.

If it's specific product friction (features, flows, interaction design), that's the most scoped response — PM and Design lead would identify the specific improvements for next quarter.

If it's all three, or no single dominant cause, leadership needs to sequence investments based on reversibility and impact rather than trying to solve everything at once.
