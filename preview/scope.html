Executive Summary

Strategic Summary

Whether Google Home can improve quality of life for people with Alzheimer's depends on understanding how cognitive decline reshapes daily routines and social connection — neither of which is well-documented in the context of voice-assisted technology in the home.

For the Designer

Where assumptions about voice technology and Alzheimer's diverge from likely reality, who the research must include, and what existing behaviors the design must account for.

Where Assumptions and Reality Diverge

What the team assumesWhat may actually be true
People with Alzheimer's can initiate voice interactions independentlyInitiation may require caregiver prompting or environmental cues — the interaction model may need to be device-initiated
"Daily task management" means reminders and calendarsThe relevant tasks are more embodied (medication, meals, hygiene) — requiring contextual, not scheduled, support
Staying in touch means making calls or sending messagesConnection may mean ambient presence, shared routines, or caregiver-mediated contact — not self-directed communication
Voice is accessible for people with cognitive impairmentWord-finding difficulties and anxiety around technology may make open-ended voice interaction a source of frustration
ACTION REQUIRED: Designer should prepare 2–3 low-fidelity interaction concepts (device-initiated check-in, ambient sharing, caregiver-mediated reminder) as discussion probes for Week 2 fieldwork.

Hypotheses to Design Against

  1. Caregiver-established routines: People with early-to-moderate Alzheimer's rely on caregiver-established routines more than personal memory for daily tasks — the product must be configured and maintained by caregivers, not by the person with Alzheimer's.
  2. Familiar repetition over variety: Social connection is sustained through familiar, repetitive interactions (same voice, same time, same format) rather than novel contact — the interaction model should prioritize consistency over flexibility.
  3. Transition-triggered need: The moments of greatest need occur during transitions (waking, mealtimes, caregiver departure) — the product should be context-triggered, not always-available.

Who We Are Designing For

SegmentBehavior definitionDesign implication
Early-stage Alzheimer'sAware of memory loss, still partially self-managing, anxious about declineProduct must support without signaling incompetence — dignity is a design constraint

Research Plan

Existing Signals to Review First

  1. Published literature on voice assistant use by older adults and people with cognitive impairment
  2. Google's internal research on Google Home accessibility and elder use cases
  3. Alzheimer's Association's existing data on daily living challenges and caregiver burden
  4. Comparable pilots: Amazon Alexa dementia care projects, UK NHS voice assistant trials
FLAG: If Google's internal accessibility research is unavailable, the team is building without knowing the platform's known limitations. Escalate to Google partner contact by Day 2.

Research Questions

Primary: What are the highest-potential use cases for Google Home to improve quality of life for people with Alzheimer's — in daily task management and social connection — and what evidence supports their viability?

  1. How do people with early-to-moderate Alzheimer's currently manage daily responsibilities, and where do breakdowns occur?
  2. How is social connection currently maintained, and what role could an ambient device play?
  3. What is the caregiver's role in establishing and maintaining technology-supported routines?
  4. What interaction patterns are viable given the cognitive profile of this population?
