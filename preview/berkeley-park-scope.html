Berkeley Public Park Improvement

Exec Summary

Strategic Summary

The city is framing this as an amenity problem when it is more likely an activation problem — the 13–44 demographic's absence from public parks is typically driven by social context, programming gaps, and perception of who else is there, not by the presence or absence of physical infrastructure.
ElementAnswer
What this is really aboutUnderstanding why the target demographic is not using the park now, before deciding what to change

Recommended Action The project lead should confirm whether capital budget, programming budget, or both are in scope before research begins — this determines which decisions the findings can actually inform, and scoping without it risks producing a readout that cannot connect to any fundable action.

For the Designer

Mental Models, Hypotheses & Segments

Where assumptions and user reality are most likely to diverge, and what design work must account for before concepts are developed.

Where Assumptions and Reality Diverge

What the team assumesWhat may actually be true
Adding or upgrading physical amenities drives usage among younger demographicsUsage in the 13–44 range is more often driven by social anchors, programming, and perception of who else is present — not infrastructure alone
13–44 is a coherent target audience that can be designed for togetherA 15-year-old and a 40-year-old parent have almost nothing in common behaviorally in a park context; treating them as one segment will produce design that serves neither well
People are staying away because the park lacks featuresPeople may be staying away because the park lacks a reason to come at a specific time — programming and activation gaps, not amenity gaps
Community spirit is built through shared spaceCommunity spirit in this demographic is more typically built through repeated shared experiences; events and recurring social rituals matter more than the space itself
→ Action Required Design should not begin concept development until fieldwork surfaces what the absent 13–44 sub-segments are currently doing instead of using this park — those behaviors are the design's direct competition.

Hypotheses to Design Against

Hypothesis 1
Teens (13–19) use parks as social infrastructure rather than physical activity venues — which means activity-focused improvements will underperform for this group unless a social anchor (gathering space, visibility, informal programming) is built in alongside them.
Hypothesis 2
Adults 25–44 perceive the park as intended for a different user (younger children, older adults, or unhoused residents) and self-exclude without testing it — which means design must signal that this space is for them specifically, not just add generic amenities.
Hypothesis 3
The absent segments are not absent because of what the park lacks, but because no recurring reason to arrive at a specific time exists — which means activation programming (scheduled, repeating, low-commitment events) may unlock usage more than any physical change.

Who We Are Designing For

SegmentBehavior definitionDesign implication
The social activator (13–44)Organizes informal group activity — pickup sports, group runs, casual meetups — and brings others with themDesign must give this person a reliable, flexible surface to activate; they are the multiplier
The event participant (13–44)Comes for scheduled programming; does not self-generate reasons to visitNeeds infrastructure that makes events reliable: power access, flexible open space, clear signage, promotion channels
The absent teen (13–19)Present in the neighborhood but not in the park; using commercial spaces, streets, or adjacent spaces for social timeMust be surfaced through off-site fieldwork; their current alternative is what design must compete with or integrate
The habitual solo userAlready visiting — runs, walks, exercises aloneNot the target for increasing usage; do not over-index design to their existing behavior at the expense of other segments

Workarounds That Already Exist

⚠ Flag Workarounds are unknown at this stage. Fieldwork must surface what the 13–44 demographic currently does instead of using this park — other parks, private gyms, commercial spaces, streets, or private homes. Designing concepts before workarounds are understood creates friction the team will discover after investment.

Constraints, Irreversible Decisions & Build Implications

What capital planning and facilities teams need to know before committing to build decisions.

Constraints That Affect the Build

ConstraintImpact on build
4-week research timeline precedes any build commitmentNo infrastructure design should begin until findings are in — parallel-tracking risks building for the wrong priority
City permitting and procurement timelinesCapital improvements may take 12–24 months from decision to implementation; programming changes can move in weeks — these are not equivalent options
ADA and municipal code complianceAny new structure, surface, or fixture must meet accessibility requirements regardless of what research recommends
Budget category constraints (capital vs. operating)Physical improvements and programming are funded differently; findings must connect to the correct budget category or they cannot be acted on
⚠ Flag If the capital improvement budget is already allocated to a specific category, that constraint must be disclosed before research begins — scoping findings against a decision that has already been made produces theater, not direction.

What Is Irreversible

  • Hardscape installation (courts, paths, stages, permanent structures) — costly to modify once poured or built
  • Electrical and utility infrastructure — requires trenching; not reversible without significant cost
  • Removal of existing trees or green space — ecological and community impact; may require environmental review
  • Any commitment made publicly to a specific improvement before research concludes

What Research Cannot Answer

📋 Context Research will identify what the target demographic needs and what barriers exist. It will not answer what is structurally feasible on this site, what subsurface conditions allow, what the permitting timeline is for specific improvement categories, or what the city's maintenance capacity can realistically support. Those questions belong to facilities and capital planning.

What Would Change the Build

If research finds thisFacilities / capital planning should do this
Primary barrier is lack of programming, not infrastructurePrioritize operating budget for activation over capital budget for physical amenities
Teens need social gathering space, not activity equipmentShift design toward flexible hardscape and seating clusters rather than fixed activity stations
Adults 25–44 are deterred by safety perception or poor lightingPrioritize lighting and sightline improvements before any amenity addition

→ Action Required Facilities lead should document which improvement categories are within the current capital budget scope before Week 2 fieldwork begins — findings will be mapped directly to fundable decisions, and this mapping cannot happen without knowing what is in scope.
Research Plan

Full Study Design

For whoever is running the study.

Existing Signals to Review First

  1. Park foot traffic counts or usage data held by Berkeley Parks & Recreation (if instrumented)
  2. Event attendance records for any programming previously held at this park
  3. Community feedback already on record: 311 service requests, Parks & Rec public comment logs, neighborhood association meeting notes, NextDoor or community social media posts referencing the park
  4. Demographic data for neighborhoods within walking distance — to understand whether the 13–44 target is well-represented nearby or requires a different recruitment strategy
⚠ Flag If none of these signals exist, that absence is itself a finding — the city has been making park decisions without behavioral data, which raises the risk that past investments have also missed the target demographic. Name this in the readout.

Research Questions

Primary question: What prevents residents aged 13–44 in the surrounding neighborhood from using this park regularly, and what specific change would make them more likely to visit?

  1. Which sub-segments within 13–44 are currently absent, and what are they doing instead? Informs: who to design for and what the design competes with
  2. Which of the four stated goals is most constrained by a behavioral or environmental barrier that a park change could address? Informs: which improvement category to fund first
  3. What programming or activation, if any, would bring the absent segments in on a recurring basis? Informs: whether capital or operating budget is the higher-leverage investment
  4. What signals in the current park environment cause the 13–44 demographic to self-exclude or feel the space is not for them? Informs: design language, activation framing, and communication
