Step 2 — Mental Model Gap

Where Assumptions and Reality Diverge

This section names where the team's assumptions and user reality may diverge — that gap is what the research tests.

What the team or industry assumesWhat may actually be true
The park is underused because it lacks amenities or programming People may avoid the park due to safety perception, lack of social context, or low awareness — not missing features
Ages 13–44 is a coherent audience with shared needs This range spans teenagers, young adults, and parents with children — groups with fundamentally different use cases and barriers
More events build community spirit (conventional parks programming assumption) Passive improvements — lighting, seating, landscaping — may drive daily use more than events, which produce short-term spikes without sustained behavior change
Physical activity infrastructure is the primary driver of usage for this demographic Perceived safety and social belonging may be stronger motivators than courts or fitness equipment
→ Action Required
Project lead should align stakeholders on which age sub-segment is the primary focus before research is designed. A single study treating ages 13–44 as one group will produce findings too broad to drive a prioritization decision.
Step 3 — Hypotheses

Three Positions to Test

Each hypothesis surfaces an assumption the team may be carrying. Alignment on priority is required before fieldwork is scheduled.

  • 1
    People ages 13–24 avoid the park not because of missing features, but because it is not perceived as a relevant social space for their peer group — which means design and programming must address belonging and identity, not amenities alone.
  • 2
    Adults ages 25–44 do not use the park regularly because it fails to support multiple simultaneous needs (fitness, child-friendliness, socializing) in one visit — which means a single improvement category will not be sufficient to shift their behavior.
  • 3
    Event attendance already outpaces casual daily use, and the real gap is non-event repeat behavior — which means community spirit initiatives anchored in events will produce short-term spikes without sustained usage change.
Step 4 — Research Approach

What We Will and Will Not Do

This section explains method selection given the specific constraints of this project — 4 weeks, unspecified budget, and a minor audience requirement.

MethodStatusReason
Contextual intercept interviews at the park ✓ Prioritized Fastest access to current users; surfaces actual use patterns and barriers without recruitment lag in a 4-week window
Brief intercept survey (5–7 questions) ✓ Prioritized Quantifies visit frequency and top barriers across sub-segments; reaches near-park non-visitors passively
Desk research — city usage data and prior studies ✓ Prioritized May already answer several questions; prevents duplicate investment; must be completed before fieldwork begins
In-depth remote interviews with non-users ✗ Deprioritized Recruiting confirmed non-users within 4 weeks is high-risk; intercepts and survey provide the essential signal
Ethnographic observation ✗ Deprioritized Valuable but time-intensive; not feasible alongside primary methods in this timeline
📋 Context
This approach assumes low-to-moderate budget. If incentivized recruitment is approved, add a brief online screener to access confirmed non-users from the 13–24 segment — the hardest group to reach through intercepts alone.

Step 6 — Constraints

What Is Achievable in 4 Weeks

These constraints determine what is achievable — and where the plan will break if not addressed before fieldwork begins.

  • Timeline: 4 weeks. Realistic allocation: Week 1 — desk research and protocol development. Weeks 2–3 — fieldwork. Week 4 — synthesis and readout.
  • Audience includes minors: Consent and safeguarding protocols are prerequisites, not parallel workstreams.
  • Budget unspecified: Method selection assumes low-to-moderate budget. Incentivized recruitment of non-users is not assumed.
  • Four unresolved goals: Without pre-fieldwork prioritization, the study will produce findings too diffuse to support a clear decision.

Step 7 — Existing Signals

What to Review Before Fieldwork Is Designed

These sources should be reviewed before any fieldwork is designed — several questions may already be answered.

  • City of Berkeley parks department usage data — permit applications, foot traffic counts, maintenance logs for this site
  • Prior community needs assessments or park master plans for this specific location
  • Service requests and complaints submitted to the parks department related to this park
  • Feedback from local schools, recreation programs, or community organizations that serve the 13–24 segment
⚠ Flag
No existing signals were provided in the brief. The research team must formally request this data from the parks department in Week 1. Fieldwork should not begin until this review is complete — or explicitly documented as unavailable and labeled as an assumption in the research plan.
Step 8 — Research Questions

What We Need to Answer

These questions, if answered, directly inform the prioritization decision named in Layer 1.

Primary Question
What prevents people ages 13–44 from using this park more regularly — and which single improvement would most change that behavior?
  • Q1
    What does each age sub-segment within 13–44 currently use the park for, and what do they report as missing or off-putting?
  • Q2
    What are the primary perceived barriers: safety, awareness, amenities, social context, programming, or other?
  • Q3
    What would make someone in this age range visit more frequently — a physical change, a recurring activity, or a social draw?
  • Q4
    Does event attendance currently translate into repeat non-event usage — and does that pattern differ by age sub-segment?

Step 11 — Stakeholder and Action Plan

Who Acts and When

Research without a named owner for each recommendation does not produce decisions — it produces reports.

  • Who this plan is for: City of Berkeley parks department, project lead
  • Who must align before research starts: Parks department data team (usage signals); legal or compliance lead (minor consent protocol); community liaisons already engaged with the 13–24 segment
  • What happens after: Week 4 readout to project lead and parks department, followed by a prioritization workshop mapping findings to specific improvement options and a Phase 1 scope decision
  • Relationship move this enables: Positions the research function as a partner in evidence-based public investment — demonstrates that community input shaped the outcome, building credibility for future city engagement work

Closing Statement

A park improvement program that proceeds with four unresolved goals and a 31-year age range as its audience will distribute investment across changes too incremental to shift behavior in any segment — and will have no basis for evaluating whether it succeeded.
